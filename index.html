Design Principles
01
PRODUCTIVE FRICTION

Discomfort is intentional. Rough edges and visual tension create the sensation of something real — made by hands, not algorithms.

02
CONTROLLED DISORDER

Chaos within a system. Every element of decay is curated. The smudge is precisely placed. Authenticity is manufactured.

03
INSTITUTIONAL IRONY

The language of power turned against itself. Official formats subverted through aesthetic choices that reveal their own artifice.

04
MATERIAL HONESTY

Exposed substrates. Visible structure. The grid is not hidden — it is the message. Infrastructure made aesthetic.

05
TEMPORAL COMPRESSION

Simultaneous past and present. New things made to appear worn. Time is collapsed and layered rather than linear.

06
SIGNAL VS NOISE

The aesthetic weaponises distraction. Information competes with texture. Difficulty of reading becomes part of reading.

Design Principles
01
ANTI-ORNAMENT

Every decorative element that doesn't carry information is a lie told to the user. Delete it without ceremony.

02
SEMANTIC TRUTH

HTML elements carry meaning. A heading is a heading. A list is a list. Div soup is an aesthetic choice — usually the wrong one.

03
HONEST INTERACTION

If something is a button, it looks like a button. If it's a link, it's blue and underlined. No deceptive affordances.

04
LOAD SPEED AS ETHIC

Every kilobyte transferred costs the user time, data, and energy. Minimising payload is a moral imperative, not a technical challenge.

05
CONTENT PRIMACY

The words matter. The numbers matter. The images matter. Everything else is furniture. If the content isn't there, design cannot save it.

06
STRUCTURE IS STYLE

A well-structured document rendered in default browser styles is more readable than most designed websites. The browser knows best.

Feature Language — The Minimal Interface
Temporal Refinement

Every design iteration removes rather than adds. The final form carries only what could not be eliminated. Time reveals what lasts.

Material Intelligence

The surface communicates the interior. Anodised aluminium. Optical glass. Ceramic. Material choice is not decoration — it is argument.

Directed Attention

When space is generous, the eye knows exactly where to go. Hierarchy without hierarchy. Navigation without instruction. Clarity as luxury.

Design Principles
01
LUXURY OF SPACE

Generous padding is not waste — it is a statement of resource and confidence. Only brands that can afford restraint are restrained.

02
INVISIBLE CRAFT

The finest work is imperceptible. Micro-adjustments in kerning, sub-pixel rendering, and optical corrections that you feel but cannot name.

03
WEIGHT AS SIGNAL

Font weight communicates price point. Ultra-light says premium. Bold says affordable. The hierarchy is understood without being stated.

04
SINGULAR ACCENT

One gold detail. One warm tone in an otherwise cold palette. The exception proves the rule and gives the eye something to return to.

05
SYSTEMIC THINKING

No element exists in isolation. Every radius, every shadow, every transition shares a logic. The system makes the individual decisions.

06
EARNED SIMPLICITY

Simple is not easy. The appearance of effortlessness requires extraordinary effort. Every simple design contains its own deleted complexity.

Design Principles
01
HORROR VACUI

The fear of empty space. Every surface is an opportunity. To leave space unfilled is to leave a canvas unfinished — a statement unmade.

02
LAYERED DEPTH

Digital Baroque thrives in the z-axis. Elements overlap, bleed through, cast light on each other. The composition has physical depth.

03
DRAMATIC CONTRAST

Extreme light and shadow, ultra-thin strokes against bold masses, whispered typeset against proclamation. Contrast creates drama.

04
GOLD AS GRAMMAR

Gilded accents are not decoration — they are punctuation. They mark the most important moment in the composition.

05
MOTION AS MEANING

Digital Baroque is never still. Particles drift. Gradients breathe. Ornaments rotate. The composition lives in the dimension of time.

06
SACRED GEOMETRY

Beneath the ornament lies mathematical order. The chaos is structured. The patterns repeat. The system supports the spectacle.

Language of Absence
The Uncanny
Familiar enough to recognise. Wrong enough to unsettle. The liminal aesthetic exploits the gap between memory and experience — the place that should not exist but clearly does.
Human Absence
The chairs arranged as though someone just left. The lights still on. The coffee still warm. Liminal spaces are defined by the negative space of human presence — the ghost of occupancy.
Temporal Suspension
These spaces exist outside of time. They do not progress. The corridor does not lead anywhere. The waiting room waits without waiting for anything in particular.
Fluorescent Grammar
The particular quality of fluorescent light on industrial carpet. The colour temperature that belongs to no hour of day — not warm, not cool. Institutional. Honest. Slightly wrong.
Design Principles
01
ABSENT PRESENCE

Design for spaces that feel inhabited but aren't. Objects that imply use. Arrangements that suggest departure.

02
FAMILIAR WRONGNESS

Every element should be recognisable. The wrongness comes from context, combination, or scale — not from the unfamiliar.

03
FLUORESCENT TRUTH

Industrial lighting reveals rather than flatters. The liminal aesthetic embraces the honest ugliness of utility lighting.

04
INFINITE CORRIDOR

The space should suggest continuation beyond the frame. The viewer should feel that the hallway extends forever in both directions.

05
TEMPORAL AMBIGUITY

No clocks. No windows. No indicators of hour or season. The space exists in an eternal administrative present.

06
CARPET AS COSMOS

The carpet pattern is the universe. Its repetition is infinity. Its staining is time. Do not underestimate the carpet.

Design Principles
01
FLATNESS AS TRUTH

Screens are flat. Simulating depth is a lie. Post-internet design accepts the two-dimensional nature of digital media without apology.

02
COPY AS ORIGINAL

In networked culture, the copy circulates further than the original. The screenshot becomes the artwork. The remix is the text.

03
PLATFORM AWARENESS

Design acknowledges its distribution channel. An image made for Instagram is shaped by Instagram. The container is part of the content.

04
IRONIC DISTANCE

Nothing is presented at face value. Every aesthetic choice carries self-awareness of its own construction. Earnestness is a disguise.

05
SPEED OF CULTURE

The aesthetic must survive at scroll speed. Complexity is lost. Legibility and impact at thumbnail resolution are the primary requirements.

06
ARCHIVE CONSCIOUSNESS

Everything is already documented. Design is produced with the knowledge that it will be screenshotted, archived, and decontextualised.

Design is direct action. The poster is a weapon. The typeface is a political choice. Every design decision carries consequence in the real world. Tactical Media refuses the separation between aesthetics and ethics — the medium is the message and the message must reach everyone.

Action Framework
ACTION 01
CULTURE JAMMING

Appropriating corporate visual language and subverting its message. Using the tools of power against power. The billboard speaks but says something different.

ACTION 02
DÉTOURNEMENT

Turning the spectacle against itself. Taking existing media — advertisements, logos, broadcasts — and rerouting their meaning toward critique or resistance.

ACTION 03
RADICAL LEGIBILITY

Communication that reaches everyone regardless of literacy level, class, or context. Bold. Simple. Immediate. The message survives a glance at 60mph.

ACTION 04
DEMOCRATIC PRODUCTION

The tools of production must be accessible. Risograph, photocopy, spray paint, free software. Tactical Media refuses the gatekeeping of professional tools.

Communiqué — The Zine Tradition

The zine is the original independent media. Photocopied, stapled, distributed by hand. No editorial board. No advertiser. No algorithm. The tactical designer understands that the means of production shape the message — and chooses their tools deliberately.

Design for the street. The flyer must read at arm's length. The poster must communicate before the eye fully focuses. The sticker must work at thumbnail size on a phone screen. Every medium has its constraints; tactical design weaponises those constraints.

Speed is political. A design that takes three months to produce is useless to a movement that moves in days. Tactical Media celebrates the rough edge of the quickly made — the spray stencil, the photocopied face, the stamp on newsprint.

Design Principles
01
URGENCY FIRST

When the situation is urgent, the design must communicate urgency. Refinement that delays is refinement that betrays.

02
DEMOCRATIC ACCESS

If the tools required to create or consume the design are not widely available, the design has already failed its political purpose.

03
APPROPRIATION

The existing language of power is a resource. Corporate logos, government fonts, official templates — all material for subversion.

04
SPEED OF PRODUCTION

The stencil and spray can are not limitations but specifications. Design that can be reproduced in minutes by anyone, anywhere.

05
MEDIUM AWARENESS

The sidewalk, the dumpster, the underpass, the bus stop — these are not inferior gallery spaces. They are the target audience.

06
NETWORK TACTICS

Design that spreads itself. The postcard is sent. The sticker travels. The PDF is shared. Virality as political strategy, not accident.
